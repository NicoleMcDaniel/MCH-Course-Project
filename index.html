Maternal and Child Health Course Project

Maternal Stress During Pregnancy and ADHD Symptoms in Children

Course: Maternal and Child Health 100Created by: Nicole McDaniel

Background and Importance

Maternal stress during pregnancy is an important MCH topic because pregnancy is an extremely sensitive time that affects both the pregnant person's health as well as how the fetus develops. Stress during pregnancy can come from many different things, like chronic stress, depression, major life events, or relationship conflict, which means it is something that can affect pregnant individuals from all kinds of backgrounds.

Because the fetal brain is developing throughout pregnancy, the prenatal period is considered a sensitive time where early exposures can have lasting effects on a child's development. Researchers study prenatal stress as one of these possible early exposures because cortisol, which is one of the body's main stress hormones, can pass through the placenta and may affect how the fetus develops its own stress response system (Rakers et al., 2017). Researchers also study whether prenatal stress may be connected to ADHD symptoms in children, since ADHD involves brain regions that support attention, memory, and self regulation, which are the same areas that develop and may be sensitive to early exposures during pregnancy.

This is why support during pregnancy should not only focus on medical checkups, but also on mental health and basic needs like stable housing, food security, and access to care. Reducing stress during pregnancy can be an early form of prevention that supports both the pregnant individual and the developing child, and may help lower the risk of ADHD symptoms later in childhood.

Research Question

How does maternal stress during pregnancy influence ADHD symptoms in children ages 3 to 15?

Stress during pregnancy is common, with research showing that up to 70% of pregnant women report symptoms of stress and anxiety during pregnancy (Pascal et al., 2023). However, stress does not affect everyone in the same way. Some pregnant people face more chronic stress because of poverty, racism, discrimination, unsafe housing, lack of transportation, limited prenatal care, or lack of support.

This project focuses on ADHD as one possible outcome shaped by these early stress exposures. ADHD is one of the most common neurodevelopmental disorders in children (CDC, 2024a), affecting roughly 7 million children ages 3 to 17 in 2022 (CDC, 2024c). It can involve inattention, hyperactivity, impulsive behavior, or a combination of these symptoms (CDC, 2024b). These challenges can affect a child's learning, relationships, and daily routines. Because ADHD can look different from child to child, this project looks at ADHD related symptoms broadly, not only children who have received a diagnosis.

Learning Objectives

  1. Apply the socioecological model to explain how maternal stress during pregnancy is shaped by individual, relationship, community, and system level factors.
  2. Describe how maternal stress during pregnancy may be linked to ADHD symptoms in children.
  3. Identify the HPA axis as a potential biological pathway linking prenatal stress to changes in child neurodevelopment.

Research Findings

Research Findings

Research does not show that maternal stress during pregnancy is the only cause of ADHD. ADHD is a complex disorder and can be influenced by genetics and the environment. However, several peer reviewed studies suggest that maternal stress, anxiety, depression, perceived stress, and stressful life events during pregnancy may be associated with higher risk of developing ADHD symptoms or ADHD diagnosis in children.

Prenatal stress is linked to ADHD symptoms in children.

A 2026 cohort study published in JAACAP Open analyzed data from the ECHO Program to determine the association between maternal prenatal perceived stress and ADHD in their children. The study included a racially and ethnically diverse sample of 6,080 mother-child dyads across 17 cohort sites, with children ranging from 3 to 15 years old (Jones et al., 2026).

Children whose mothers reported high perceived stress during pregnancy had 2.46 times the odds of receiving an ADHD diagnosis from a healthcare provider compared to children whose mothers reported low perceived stress. The results were also adjusted for maternal age, prenatal tobacco and alcohol use, and maternal education. The connection was even stronger when researchers looked at children's behavior scores instead of formal diagnoses. The Child Behavior Checklist (CBCL) is a widely used tool that measures behavior problems in children. Preschool aged children whose mothers experienced high stress during pregnancy were 5.14 times more likely to score in the clinical range for ADHD symptoms on this checklist, and school aged children showed nearly identical results of 4.94 times more likely. The scores for behaviors like acting out, breaking rules, and aggression were also higher in children whose mothers reported medium or high stress during pregnancy. These findings held up even after the researchers accounted for whether the mother had depression, anxiety, ADHD, or a history of substance use. This means that prenatal stress carries its own risks separate from other maternal mental health conditions (Jones et al., 2026).

These findings also line up with what other research has shown. A review that combined results from 55 different studies found that prenatal stress, anxiety, and depression are linked to more behavior problems in children from early childhood through the teenage years (Tung et al., 2023). Earlier studies by Okano et al. (2018) and Rodriguez and Bohlin (2005) reported similar results, showing that mothers who experienced more stress during pregnancy were more likely to have children who developed ADHD or showed stronger ADHD symptoms. A broader review by Van den Bergh et al. (2020) analyzed studies showing that prenatal stress is linked not only to ADHD related symptoms, but also to a wider range of outcomes including problems with attention, learning, emotional regulation, and risk for psychiatric conditions later in life.

What This Means

Research consistently links high stress during pregnancy to an increased chance of ADHD diagnosis and ADHD symptoms in children. These results do not prove that prenatal stress directly causes ADHD, since genetics and the environment also play a part. Still, the fact that many different studies have found similar results suggests that prenatal stress is a meaningful risk factor.

Stress can take many different forms.

Prenatal stress does not look the same for everyone. Anxiety, depression, and sleep problems often overlap with everyday stress, but each one is its own part of a mother's mental health and may affect how a child's brain develops. The NINFEA birth cohort study examined 3,634 mother-child dyads in Italy. Researchers looked at whether mothers had been diagnosed with anxiety, depression, or a sleep disorder before or during pregnancy, and then measured ADHD symptoms in their children at age 4 (Vizzini et al., 2018).

After adjusting for gender, first born status, maternal age, education, alcohol consumption, and smoking during pregnancy, the researchers found that children's total ADHD scores at age 4 were significantly higher among those whose mothers had a lifetime history of anxiety (17.1% higher score), depression (17.5% higher), or sleep disorders (35.7% higher). Each condition was looked at on its own, which means anxiety, depression, and sleep problems each appear to raise the risk of ADHD symptoms independently and not just because they tend to occur together (Vizzini et al., 2018).

The HPA axis links prenatal stress to ADHD.

One way that stress during pregnancy may affect a fetus' brain is through the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis. The HPA axis is the body's main stress response system. When activated, it triggers the release of corticotropin releasing hormone (CRH) from the hypothalamus, which signals the pituitary to release adrenocorticotropic hormone (ACTH), which in turn activates the adrenal glands to produce cortisol, the body's main stress hormone.

In a 2023 review, Makris et al. (2022) explains how the HPA axis acts as a main pathway through which stress during pregnancy may shape a child's brain development. Normally, the placenta produces an enzyme called 11β-HSD2 that breaks down most of the mother's cortisol before it reaches the baby, acting like a filter. But stress during pregnancy can lower the activity of this enzyme, leaving the fetus exposed to higher levels of cortisol during important stages of brain development.

When cortisol does reach the developing brain, it can affect regions that are important for attention, behavior, and self regulation which are the same areas that are affected in ADHD. Animal studies have shown that high prenatal cortisol exposure can change how neurons grow and lead to a smaller hippocampus, a brain region involved in memory and stress regulation (Makris et al., 2022). Another study conducted by Buss found that higher maternal cortisol in early pregnancy was associated with a larger right amygdala in girls and higher levels of emotional problems in girls (Buss et al., 2012). Because these are the same brain regions involved in attention and self control, researchers believe that prenatal stress may affect how the child's HPA axis develops, which could be one reason it is linked to later ADHD symptoms.

Furthermore, children exposed to high levels of stress before birth often show higher baseline cortisol, stronger reactions to stress later in life, and changes in the brain receptors that normally help shut off the stress response (Van den Bergh et al., 2020). A study of 173 mother-infant dyads found that mothers who reported more stress during pregnancy had infants with altered cortisol responses to everyday stressful events (Tollenaar et al., 2010), and a separate study found that babies exposed to higher cortisol in the womb showed different patterns of stress reactivity at 17 months old, suggesting that the prenatal environment may set the baby's stress system in lasting ways (O'Connor et al., 2012).

What This Means

The HPA axis is one explanation for how prenatal stress can influence a child's brain development. It can change how the fetus is exposed to cortisol, which may shape the brain regions and stress response system involved in attention, behavior, and self regulation later in childhood.

Prenatal stress is a risk factor.

Some children may be more sensitive to prenatal stress than others because of differences in their genes. O'Donnell et al. (2017) studied data from 6,969 mother-child dyads in a large UK study, and then ran the same analysis in a separate group of 425 mother-child dyads from Finland to check the results. The researchers focused on a gene called COMT, which helps the body control dopamine, a brain chemical involved in attention and behavior. They found that when mothers had higher anxiety during pregnancy, their children showed more ADHD symptoms and weaker working memory, but only in children who had one specific version of the COMT gene. Children with other versions of the same gene were less affected (O'Donnell et al. 2017). This shows that the effects of prenatal stress can depend on a child's genetic background, which helps explain why some children exposed to prenatal stress may develop ADHD symptoms while others may not.

It is also important to remember that ADHD has many different causes, and genetics plays a major role. Research suggests that 70 to 80% of the differences in ADHD risk between children can be explained by genetic factors (Faraone & Larsson, 2018). Stress during pregnancy is just one factor that can affect a child's development, in addition to genetics, the environment after birth, and a family's social life.

MCH Lens: Disparities and Solutions

MCH Lens: Disparities and Solutions

Looking at maternal stress through a MCH lens, shows that it is a public health issue that comes from more than just the pregnant person themselves. It is something that is shaped by social, economic, environmental, and healthcare conditions that affect a pregnant individual long before and during pregnancy. This perspective shifts the focus away from blaming mothers and toward looking at what creates stress and what could help reduce it.

Some pregnant individuals face more stress than others.

Prenatal stress can affect anyone and everyone, but some pregnant individuals face higher levels of chronic stress because of poverty, racism, discrimination, unsafe housing, food insecurity, lack of transportation, or limited access to healthcare. These stressors can build up over time and make pregnancy even more difficult.

Because this stress can be due to larger social inequities, prenatal stress is a MCH disparities issue. Using a disparity lens helps show us that maternal stress is because of much more than how someone manages it. Telling a pregnant individual to "reduce stress in their daily life" does not help much when they are dealing with financial issues, discrimination, or limited access to care. Therefore, public health should focus on creating upstream policies to change the conditions that cause this chronic stress, not just expecting individuals to manage it on their own.

Maternal stress comes from multiple levels.

The SEM helps explain how prenatal stress can come from many levels. At the individual level, stress may involve mental health, pregnancy complications, sleep problems, or personal trauma. At the relationship level, stress may come from lack of partner support, family conflict, or partner violence. At the community level, stress may come from unsafe neighborhoods, unstable housing, limited transportation, or lack of nearby healthcare services. At the systems level, stress may be shaped by poverty, racism, discrimination, insurance barriers, lack of paid leave, and limited access to mental health care.

Looking at this research through a socioecological model shows that prenatal stress is not only about the pregnant person's emotions. It is also about the environment surrounding the pregnancy. This is so important for MCH because the environment a mother is in matters just as much for her and her child's health as biology does.

MCH should focus on support, not blame.

Addressing prenatal stress could improve maternal wellbeing and possibly support healthier child development. One solution is to screen for stress, anxiety, depression, and safety concerns during prenatal visits. Screening should not just identify problems but should also connect pregnant individuals to support like counseling, social workers, housing resources, food programs, transportation support, and community care.

Furthermore, MCH programs should treat mental health support as part of prenatal care. If stress during pregnancy is linked to later child development, then prevention should begin before birth. This means improving access to affordable prenatal care, mental health care, and social support during pregnancy. It also means reducing barriers that make the stress worse, especially for low income communities and communities experiencing discrimination.
